## Thread 10: The AgentRank score explained

**Educational thread about our methodology**

---

**Tweet 1/6**
There are 25,632 MCP servers indexed at AgentRank.

How do we score them? Eight signals. Here's what each one measures and why it matters:

**Tweet 2/6**
Dependents — 22% of score
How many other projects depend on this one?

This is the strongest signal we have. A tool that other tools build on is a tool that works. Dependency data comes from npm, PyPI, and GitHub's dependency graph.

**Tweet 3/6**
Freshness — 20%
When was the last commit? Is the repo still alive?

A tool with 10,000 stars but no commits since 2024 is a liability. Freshness penalizes abandoned projects regardless of historical popularity.

Issue Health — 20%
Ratio of open to closed issues + how fast maintainers respond.

A low close rate means bugs pile up and nobody fixes them.

**Tweet 4/6**
Downloads — 13%
npm and PyPI weekly download counts.

Real usage signal. Projects downloaded thousands of times per week are embedded in production pipelines, not just starred on GitHub.

Stars — 10%
Yes, only 10%. Stars are a popularity signal, not a quality signal.

We include them but don't overweight them.

**Tweet 5/6**
Contributors — 8%
More contributors means more eyes on the code, faster issue resolution, and a project that doesn't die when one person gets busy.

Description Quality — 4%
Is the project actually documented? A great tool with a useless README doesn't help anyone.

License Health — 3%
Open source with a clear license. Matters more when you're building on top of it commercially.

**Tweet 6/6**
Every signal is normalized 0-100 and combined into a single score.

A score of 98 means the project is exceptional across almost all signals. A score of 29 (the average) means it's new, barely maintained, or both.

Full methodology at agentrank-ai.com/methodology
